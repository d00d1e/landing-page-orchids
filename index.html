Origins

The world of orchids—with its beauty, dramatic shapes, and colors—has fascinated plant enthusiasts for centuries. These magnificent flowers first appeared nearly 200 million years ago and have since spread from the equator to the Arctic Circle and from the Himalayas to Australia. Researchers dated orchids from the Jurassic period to the Mesozoic era (195–136 million years ago) and the Cenozoic period (64 million years ago).

All Shapes, Sizes and Colors

There’s more to the orchid than meets the eye and its beauty is quite hard to deny. With a delicate, sculptural beauty and historical rarity, these exotic blooms carry an unrivaled symbol of refinement, luxury and mystery.

As one of the largest flowering plant families, every orchid species has a unique look that can range from simple and elegant to many-petaled and exotic -- but no other flower looks quite like an orchid. From the large, flat blooms of the Phalaenopsis to the bright daffodil-like flowers of the Cymbidium to the daintily fashioned three-petaled Paphiopedilum blooms, these showy and remarkable flowers are aesthetically pleasing and makes them a favorite among plant enthusiasts alike.

Caring for

Orchids

Basic Care

Many orchids can be easy to grow if you take some time to learn what they need. They are epiphytes which means that they do not grow in dirt but rather by hanging on to the bark of trees. Orchids require little care once all their basic needs are met such as light, temperature, and humidity.

Orchids require moist, well-draining conditions but should be allowed to dry out some between waterings. Indoor orchid plants also need adequate humidity, about fifty to seventy percent. Temperature is also important for indoor orchid care. While orchids tolerate cooler or warmer temperatures throughout their normal growing season, they need to be about 15 degrees (8 degrees C) cooler at night than during the day in order to bloom sufficiently.

Additional orchid care tips include repotting, which is normally done every other year. If your orchids suddenly stop blooming but have suitable light, temperature, and humidity, then repotting may be necessary. Also, keep an eye out for signs of pests or disease. Orchids are occasionally affected by mealybugs, scale, and aphids. These can usually be washed off or treated with insecticidal soap.
